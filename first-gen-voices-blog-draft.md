# What I Learned Building *First-Gen Voices* (So Far)

I started *First-Gen Voices* in 2024 because I kept hearing first-gen students share really useful stories in private conversations, while official campus information often sounded certain and tidy. Real experience usually feels less certain than that. I wanted to make a thoughtful place for those stories without pretending any one story speaks for every first-gen student.

I’m the founder and producer, and I do the recording, editing, cover art, and release scheduling. So far I’ve released 15 episodes and passed 2,000 downloads. I’m sharing a few of the biggest lessons because if you’re another student creator, I hope this saves you some stress.

At the beginning, I overplanned. In Episode 1, I had 14 questions in my outline and kept looking down at the page instead of listening. The best part of that interview happened after I stopped following the outline so tightly. By Episode 2, I used a shorter outline and got better answers. I also learned that silence feels much longer while recording than it does when you listen back later. In Episode 3, I jumped in too fast. Waiting a little longer usually gets better responses.

I also had to get better at handling sensitive topics. By Episode 4, I noticed how easy it is to accidentally make one person’s story sound universal, so I started being more explicit in intros: this is one person’s experience. Later, for Episode 9, I sent the topic outline before recording so there were no surprises. That helped a lot. I also learned that a conversation can still be useful without including every personal detail.

On the production side, perfectionism almost made me miss the point. Episode 5 went out late because I kept changing tiny edits. That was my reminder that clear and respectful beats perfect. I wrote a production checklist (audio levels, remove obvious distractions, listen through, export, artwork, schedule), and by Episode 6 I could follow the process instead of relying on memory. In Episode 12, during a busy week, I used that same checklist and still released on time. For me, consistency has been more about process than motivation.

One of the biggest shifts was how I treat listener questions. By Episode 7, I was already saving listener questions as they came in. By Episode 13, I noticed repeated themes in follow-up questions and added those themes to my future list. By Episode 15, I was using my listener-question list while planning. Questions aren’t just feedback after publishing—they can shape what you explore next.

Another lesson was about endings. In Episode 14, I caught myself trying to force an inspirational close. Keeping the specific advice instead was more useful, and now I treat that as a rule when I wrap an episode.

I learned a few smaller interview habits that made a big difference: I talk more when I’m nervous, and short follow-ups are usually better. “Can you tell me more about that?” gets me farther than a long, stacked question.

If you’re building your own student project, here’s what I’d pass along:

1. Start with fewer questions and listen for follow-ups.
2. Send the topic outline ahead of time for sensitive conversations.
3. Use a production checklist instead of waiting to feel motivated.
4. Choose clear and respectful over perfect.
5. Save listener questions as future episode ideas.
6. End with specific advice instead of forcing an inspirational conclusion.

One last wording note I keep for myself: I avoid saying “giving people a voice.” People already have voices. The work is creating space to listen carefully.
