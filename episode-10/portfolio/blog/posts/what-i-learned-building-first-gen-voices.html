What I Learned Building First-Gen Voices

I started First-Gen Voices to share real first-gen stories, and these are a few of the biggest lessons that helped me keep creating with care and consistency.

I started First-Gen Voices in 2024 because I kept hearing first-gen students share really useful stories in private conversations, while official campus information often sounded certain and tidy. Real experience usually feels less certain than that. I wanted to make a thoughtful place for those stories without pretending any one story speaks for every first-gen student.

I am the founder and producer, and I handle recording, editing, cover art, and release scheduling. So far I have released 15 episodes and passed 2,000 downloads. These are a few of the biggest lessons I would share with another student creator.

Start with fewer questions and listen more

At the beginning, I overplanned. In Episode 1, I had 14 questions in my outline and kept looking down at the page instead of listening. The part I liked most happened after I stopped following the outline so tightly.

By Episode 2, I used a shorter outline and got better answers. In Episode 3, I also learned that silence feels much longer while recording than it does when listening back later. I was jumping in too fast. Waiting a little longer usually gives better responses.

Be careful with sensitive topics

By Episode 4, I noticed how easy it is to accidentally make one person’s story sound universal, so I started saying clearly in the intro that this is one person’s experience.

For Episode 9, I sent the topic outline before recording so there were no surprises. That helped a lot. I also learned I did not need every personal detail for the conversation to be useful.

Choose consistency over perfection

Episode 5 went out late because I kept changing tiny edits. That reminded me that clear and respectful beats perfect.

I wrote a production checklist: audio levels, remove obvious distractions, listen through, export, artwork, and schedule. By Episode 6, I could follow the process instead of relying on memory. In Episode 12, during a busy week, I used the same checklist and still released it. For me, consistency has been more about process than motivation.

Treat listener questions as planning input

By Episode 7, I was saving listener questions as they came in. By Episode 13, I noticed repeated follow-up themes and added those themes to my future list. By Episode 15, I used that listener-question list while planning.

Questions are not just feedback after publishing. They can help decide what to explore next.

Keep endings specific

In Episode 14, I caught myself trying to force an inspirational ending. Keeping the specific advice instead was more useful, and now I treat that as a rule when I wrap an episode.

I also keep one wording reminder for myself: I avoid saying “giving people a voice.” People already have voices. The work is creating space to listen carefully.

What I would tell another student creator

  1. Start with fewer questions and listen for follow-ups.
  2. Send the topic outline ahead of time for sensitive conversations.
  3. Use a production checklist instead of waiting to feel motivated.
  4. Choose clear and respectful over perfect.
  5. Save listener questions as future episode ideas.
  6. End with specific advice instead of forcing an inspirational conclusion.
